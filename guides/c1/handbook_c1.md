## Part 1 — Assessment Scales

### How Marking Works

Tasks are marked using four subscales developed with explicit reference to the CEFR. Responses are marked on each subscale from **0 to 5**.

- Length guidelines are given for each task. Too short = inadequate range and missing information. Too long = likely irrelevance, repetition, or poor organisation. Either affects marks on the relevant subscales.
- Candidates should use one variety of English consistently (e.g. British or American spelling — not mixed within the same word).

### How Band 5 Differs from Band 3 at C1

**Communicative Achievement:** Band 5 requires *sufficient flexibility* — the candidate adapts genre conventions rather than applying them mechanically. Band 3 requires conventions to be used *effectively* but not necessarily flexibly.

**Organisation:** Band 5 requires a *variety of cohesive devices and organisational patterns with flexibility* — a coherent whole. Band 3 requires variety to *generally good effect*, but flexibility is not required throughout.

**Language:** Band 5 requires less common lexis used *effectively and precisely*, plus *full control, flexibility and sophistication* in grammar. Band 3 requires less common lexis used *appropriately* and grammar used with *control and flexibility* (but not yet full sophistication). At Band 5, errors are limited to slips or less common structures; at Band 3, occasional errors are acceptable.

## Part 2 — Glossary of Key Marking Terms

### General

**Generally** — a qualifier meaning "not in every way or instance". *Generally appropriately* is not as strong as *appropriately*.

**Flexibility** — the ability to adapt language, organisational devices, or task conventions rather than repeating the same form. Flexibility evidences better control and a wider repertoire and allows a candidate to better achieve communicative goals.

### Content

**Relevant** — related or relatable to required content points and/or task requirements.

**Target reader** — the hypothetical reader set up in the task (e.g. a magazine's readership, your English teacher).

**Informed** — the target reader is informed if content points and/or task requirements are addressed and appropriately developed. Some content points need little development (*state what X is*); others require it (*describe, explain*).

### Communicative Achievement

**Conventions of the communicative task** — genre, format, register and function. A personal letter should not be written as a formal report, should be laid out accordingly, and use the right tone for the communicative purpose.

**Holding the target reader's attention** — used in the positive sense: the quality of a text that allows a reader to derive meaning and not be distracted. Does NOT refer to texts that force close reading because they are difficult to follow or make sense of.

**Communicative purpose** — the communicative requirements as set out in the task, e.g. *make a complaint*, *suggest alternatives*.

**Straightforward ideas** — limited, usually concrete subject matter requiring simpler rhetorical devices.

**Complex ideas** — more abstract in nature, or covering a wider subject area, requiring more rhetorical resources to bring together and express.

### Organisation

**Linking words** — high-frequency cohesive vocabulary providing explicit linkage: *and, but, because, first of all, finally*.

**Cohesive devices** — more sophisticated linking words and phrases (*moreover, it may appear, as a result*) plus grammatical devices: reference pronouns, substitution (*There are two women. The one on the right…*), ellipsis, repetition.

**Organisational patterns** — less-explicit ways of achieving connection beyond the sentence level: climactic sentence ordering, parallelism, rhetorical questions to introduce new paragraphs.

### Language

**Basic vocabulary** — survival/simple transaction vocabulary.

**Everyday vocabulary** — vocabulary that comes up in common situations of a non-technical nature.

**Less common lexis** — vocabulary items that appear less often in the relevant domain; often help to express ideas more succinctly and precisely.

**Appropriacy of vocabulary** — words and phrases that fit the context of the task. *I'm very sensible to noise* → *sensible* should be *sensitive*. *Big snow* → should be *heavy snow* (collocation).

**Simple grammatical forms** — words, phrases, basic tenses and simple clauses.

**Complex grammatical forms** — noun clauses, relative and adverb clauses, subordination, passive forms, infinitives, verb patterns, modal forms, tense contrasts.

**Grammatical control** — the ability to consistently use grammar accurately and appropriately to convey intended meaning.

**Range** — the variety of words and grammatical forms used. At higher levels, candidates use a greater variety of words, fixed phrases, collocations and grammatical forms.

**Overuse** — repeatedly using the same word because the candidate lacks the resources to rephrase. (Note: unavoidable topic-forced repetition is not overuse.)

**Errors** — systematic mistakes.

**Slips** — non-systematic mistakes; the candidate has learned the item but made a one-off mistake. Where most other examples of a lexical/grammatical point are accurate, a mistake on that point is likely a slip.

**Impede communication** — getting in the way of meaning. *Meaning can still be determined* = some reader effort is required.

## Part 3 — Sample Answers with Official Scores and Commentary

### Q1 — Part 1 Essay (Facilities for local authority funding)

**Candidate A** — Essay arguing museums and parks should receive funding; concludes in favour of museums.

| Subscale | Band | Commentary |
|---|---|---|
| Content | 5 | All content relevant. Discusses two facilities (museums and parks), chooses one (museums), gives reasons for each. Final decision made in concluding paragraph. |
| Communicative Achievement | 3 | Essay conventions well used. Clear opening and strong conclusion. Register consistently formal. Clear paragraphing holds attention and communicates straightforward and more complex ideas logically. |
| Organisation | 3 | Well organised and coherent; variety of cohesive devices to generally good effect (*Nowadays; On the one hand; Moreover; For example; Therefore; On the other hand; Furthermore; All in all*). Mix of long and short sentences; some shorter ones could be combined. |
| Language | 2 | Range of relevant vocabulary not always used successfully (*In a world where true values are not respected…* adds no extra information). Range of simple and some complex structures with control. Word order and pronoun problems (*it can be organised events; there can be built*). Errors with plurals and articles do not impede communication. |

**Candidate B** — Essay choosing both sports centres and parks; links them through a shared argument about neglect.

| Subscale | Band | Commentary |
|---|---|---|
| Content | 3 | Content relevant; target reader on the whole informed. Candidate does not make a final selection between the two facilities, but justifies choosing both. |
| Communicative Achievement | 4 | Conventions of essay writing evident; attention held throughout. Opening sets context; candidate links both facilities throughout effectively. Arguments backed up with evidence from personal experience or input text. Consistent register; suitably persuasive and objective tone. |
| Organisation | 4 | Well organised and coherent. Subtle organisational patterns and cohesive devices rather than overt linking words (*Having listened to … I realised that; We see lots of kids nowadays suffering … caused by; Another reason for this is; The ones that are*). Some sentences could be connected for more fluid text, but overall effect good. |
| Language | 3 | Range of vocabulary and some less common lexis collocated appropriately (*neglected over the years; suffering from obesity; lacking in numbers; follow a sports career; quality time*). Range of simple and more complex structures with control. Slight awkwardness and few errors do not cause reader difficulty. |
