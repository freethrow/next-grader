# C1 Advanced — Writing Checklist

*Source: Cambridge University Press & Assessment, 2024*

---

## How the Writing Paper is Assessed

Four criteria are considered when marking C1 Advanced writing:

| Criterion | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Content** | You have done what the task asked. You included all required information. No irrelevant material or misinterpretation. |
| **Communicative Achievement** | The writing is appropriate for the task. Correct style and register for the communicative purpose. |
| **Organisation** | The writing is logically ordered and clearly connected. The reader can follow it easily. |
| **Language** | Good range of vocabulary and grammar, used accurately. Even if some mistakes are present, the reader can understand the text. |

---

## Self-Check Checklist

For a good piece of writing at C1 Advanced level, you should be able to answer **yes** to every question below.

### Content

- [ ] Have I covered **all** the key information required by the task?
- [ ] Have I written **only** information which is relevant to the task?
- [ ] Have I developed the basic points in the task with my own ideas?

### Communicative Achievement

- [ ] Have I achieved the main purpose(s) of the text (e.g. explaining, persuading, suggesting, apologising, comparing)?
- [ ] Have I used a suitable mix of fact and opinion?
- [ ] Have I used a suitable style and register (formal or informal) for the task?

### Organisation

- [ ] Have I used paragraphs appropriately to organise my ideas?
- [ ] Have I used other organisational features appropriately for the genre (e.g. titles, headings, openings, closings)?
- [ ] Is the connection between my ideas clear and easy for the reader to follow? (e.g. appropriate linking words, pronouns, etc.)
- [ ] Are the ideas balanced appropriately, with suitable attention and space given to each one?

### Language

- [ ] Have I used a wide range of vocabulary?
- [ ] Have I avoided repeating the same words and phrases?
- [ ] Have I used a range of simple and more complex grammatical structures?
- [ ] Have I correctly used any common phrases relevant to the specific task or topic?
- [ ] Is my use of grammar accurate?
- [ ] Is my spelling accurate?

## C1 Advanced Writing Paper — Format

**Paper 2: Writing — 1 hour 30 minutes**

| Part | Questions | Task | Format |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 (compulsory) | Essay with discursive focus | 220–260 words. Based on two points from the input. Explain which is more important and give reasons. |
| 2 | 1 (from choice of 3) | One from possible text types | 220–260 words. Clear context, topic, purpose, and target reader. Output types: letter/email, proposal, report, review. |

---

## Task Types — Key Features

### Essay (Part 1 — compulsory)
- Written for an academic tutor, often as a follow-up to a class activity (seminar, documentary, etc.).
- Select **two** bullet points from the input; base the essay on those two only.
- Main purpose: highlight relevant salient issues and support an argument with subsidiary points and reasons.
- Structure: clear introduction, developed body, appropriate conclusion.
- Opinions from the input may be used but must be paraphrased — no credit for lifted language.
- Register: formal or neutral.

### Letter / Email (Part 2)
- Written in response to the situation outlined in the task.
- Requires consistent register appropriate to the specified target reader.
- May involve: narrative, factual information, reassurance, correction, justification.
- Email conventions: opening salutation, clear paragraphing, closing phrasing.

### Proposal (Part 2)
- Written for a peer group or supervisor.
- Must make one or more suggestions, supported by factual information and evaluation, to persuade the reader of a course of action.
- Structured under headings (similar to a report); polite, persuasive register.

### Report (Part 2)
- Written for a superior or peer group.
- Content is partly factual but allows candidates to include their own ideas and experience.
- Goes beyond description: may evaluate to what extent an aim has been achieved or suggest an alternative course of action.
- Use headings where appropriate; clear, objective register.

### Review (Part 2)
- May be about a book, magazine, film, play, concert, product, or service.
- Does **not** merely describe; requires an **evaluation** of suitability for a particular purpose or audience.
- Target reader is specified — use this to choose appropriate ideas and language.
- Include opinion (positive or negative) and a recommendation.

## Key Reminders from Cambridge

- Writing the right number of words (220–260) is an integral part of task achievement. Too few words = task likely not completed. Too many = probable irrelevance or poor organisation.
- Spelling errors and faulty punctuation are not specifically penalised, but they can impede communication and affect the target reader negatively.
- American and other varieties of English are acceptable — but use one variety consistently.
- Complex language is rewarded even if minor errors result, **provided** those errors do not impede communication. Examiners credit ambition.
- Pre-learned answers are unlikely to meet the exact requirements of a specific exam task.
